Title: Off campus housing — advice for students choosing between on and off campus

Post body:
Looking for general advice on off campus housing as a college student. I'm a first-year trying to figure out whether to live on or off campus next year. I've heard mixed things and want to get a realistic picture of what off campus is actually like — costs, freedom, responsibilities, social life impact.

Comments:

- Off campus is almost universally cheaper than on campus if you have good roommates. The savings depend on how many people you split with. A 4-person house in most college towns will get you $400-600/person/month. On campus dorms with a mandatory meal plan often run $7,000-10,000/semester at many schools.

- The freedom is real and it's wonderful. You cook your own food, have people over whenever, don't have RAs checking on you. But it comes with real responsibilities that surprise a lot of first-timers: signing a lease, setting up utilities, knowing when rent is due, dealing with landlords when things break.

- On campus is usually better for social life, especially for freshmen and sophomores. The proximity to other students, the built-in community of the dorm, the ease of meeting people — that's hard to replicate off campus. By junior year you have your friend group and it matters less.

- When comparing costs, make sure you're comparing apples to apples. On-campus usually includes some form of meal plan. Off-campus you pay rent plus utilities plus all food. Add those up before deciding off-campus is cheaper.

- The commute matters more than people expect. When it's 8am in February and your class is across campus and it's 15 degrees, being 10 minutes vs 30 minutes from campus makes a real difference. And bus schedules in college towns are often unreliable.

- I moved off campus sophomore year and it changed my college experience completely. I went from spending $1,100/month (room and meal plan divided monthly) to $600/month (rent + utilities + groceries). That extra money went toward actually doing things I enjoyed. No regrets.

- One thing nobody tells you about off campus: you need to start looking WAY earlier than feels natural. In competitive markets, students sign leases for next year's housing in October or November of the current year. If you wait until spring you'll get whatever's left.

- Roommate compatibility is serious. You can live with your best friend and discover you have incompatible habits. Talk openly before signing anything about sleep schedules, cleanliness, guests, noise, and how expenses get split. A roommate agreement, while awkward to make, prevents a lot of pain.

- The biggest surprise my first off-campus year: utilities. I knew I'd pay for electricity but I didn't think about gas heat, internet, water (sometimes charged separately), and how these vary by season. Budget $80-150/person/month on top of rent for a rough estimate.

- Get renter's insurance. It's like $12/month and covers your laptop, bike, and other valuables if there's a theft, fire, or leak. Your landlord's insurance does not cover your stuff.

- Moving off campus is a life skills accelerator. You learn to cook, budget, deal with contracts, advocate for yourself with landlords, and coordinate with other adults. These skills matter and you might as well build them in a lower-stakes environment.

- For first year students: the general wisdom is stay on campus. The social investment freshman year is worth it. Move off campus when you know who you want to live with and you're ready to manage the logistics. But there's no rule — independent students who already know the area and have friends lined up do fine off campus from day one.

- Check the lease for joint and several liability. This clause means the landlord can collect the full rent from any one of you if others don't pay. This is standard in most student leases and it means your roommates' financial reliability directly affects your risk. Know who you're signing with.
