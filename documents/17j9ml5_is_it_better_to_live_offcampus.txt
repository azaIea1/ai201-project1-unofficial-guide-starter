Title: Is it better to live offcampus?

Post body:
Finishing up my freshman year and debating whether to move off campus for sophomore year. My parents are supportive either way but want me to think it through. Is it actually better? What are the downsides nobody talks about?

Comments:

- Depends what you mean by "better." Off campus is almost always cheaper. The freedom is real — you cook your own food, set your own schedule, no RA knocking on your door. But it adds real responsibilities that can catch you off guard if you've never dealt with them.

- Sophomore year is probably too early for most people unless you have a really solid group of friends ready to live with you. The social life freshman year is centered on the dorms. Moving off campus sophomore year means you miss out on a lot of that organic community building.

- I moved off sophomore year and it was the right call for me. I was paying about $9,500 a year for a double in Mountainview with the mandatory meal plan. Off campus I pay $575/month for a room in a 4BR house, plus about $80/month in utilities and food. Do the math — I'm saving close to $3,000/year even accounting for everything.

- The downsides nobody talks about: dealing with a bad landlord is genuinely stressful. Our heat went out in January and it took the landlord two weeks to fix it. On campus, facilities comes within 24 hours. You're also responsible for things like making sure the smoke detectors work, dealing with pests if the landlord ignores it, and navigating lease renewals.

- The commute isn't bad if you live on the Vestal Parkway side. BU runs shuttle buses and if you have a car or a bike it's fine. But Binghamton weather is brutal in winter. Walking a half mile in ice and wind is not fun, and bus schedules aren't always reliable.

- Living off campus made me a better student, honestly. The dorms were too social — always someone in the hall, always noise. Having a quiet space where I could actually study made a difference. My GPA went up sophomore year.

- The cooking thing sounds nice in theory but it's a lot of work. I ate really well the first month and then slowly regressed to ramen and frozen burritos because I was too busy. If the dining hall was convenient I probably would have used it more. That said, I'm saving money on food even eating like garbage because the meal plan cost so much.

- Make sure you have a really honest conversation with whoever you're planning to live with. Living with friends is not the same as hanging out with friends. Find out how they are about chores, noise, guests, late nights, splitting bills. One incompatible roommate can ruin the whole year.

- Off campus is better if: you're self-sufficient, have a solid friend group, have at least one person with a car, and you've identified a good apartment early. It's worse if: you don't have reliable roommates, you're still figuring out your social circle, or you hate administrative friction.

- One underrated thing: having a real kitchen is awesome. Being able to meal prep, have people over for dinner, actually cook — it changes the college experience in a good way. Beats standing in line at the dining hall.

- I'd say do it junior year at the earliest if you're unsure. By then you know who your actual friends are and you've had time to save up a bit. The first/last month's rent plus security deposit can be $1,500-2,000 upfront which is a real barrier if you don't plan for it.

- The biggest hidden cost is utilities in winter. Binghamton gets cold and old apartments have terrible insulation. Our gas bill was $180/month December through February. Factor that in when you're comparing costs to on-campus.
