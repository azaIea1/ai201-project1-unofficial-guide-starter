Title: Why is the difference between on campus and off campus housing so much?

Post body:
Hello, So I'm a college student who has lived independently since about 17 years old, so I qualify for a lot of grants and what not that usually help pay for my cost of living. I live in California USA.

I recently found out that depending on if you live "on campus" or "off campus", it changes your "cost of living" on your calculated financial aid SUBSTANTIALLY. (You have to select one of these with financial aid office)

I'm not joking when I say this, it is about an $11,000 difference at my school. And this is a big deal because grants and what not will be cut off or capped to a much lower amount if you select "off campus".

The large difference is kind of confusing for me. Because if I pay for an apartment or rent a room close to campus, wouldn't I still technically need to say I live "off campus"? Or does "off campus" imply that you are living with parents?

I have lived on campus before, and I did not need to pay for a meal plan or any extra fees, it was just my regular rent price for my dorm. So I'm not sure why there is such a disparity in the differences here either. Can someone help to make sense of this for me?

Comments:

- The financial aid calculation uses different "cost of attendance" budgets for on-campus vs off-campus students. On-campus usually has higher housing allowances built in because the university knows exactly what room and board costs. Off-campus is estimated based on typical local living costs. That $11,000 gap is your school's estimate of the difference between on-campus room and board and off-campus apartment + food.

- "Off campus" doesn't mean living with parents. It means living anywhere that isn't a university-operated dormitory or apartment building. Renting a room near campus is off-campus housing.

- The financial aid office uses a "cost of attendance" (COA) budget that varies by housing status. Off-campus COA at many schools is lower than on-campus COA because they assume you can find cheaper housing than the dorms. If your school's off-campus COA is $11K lower, that's affecting how much aid you can receive to cover living costs.

- At Binghamton and many SUNY schools, the same pattern applies. On-campus room and board is priced at whatever the university sets (often $10,000-15,000/year). Off-campus COA is estimated lower based on regional apartment costs. This can result in less aid for off-campus students even if their actual costs are similar.

- You can usually appeal your COA if your actual off-campus costs are higher than what the school estimates. Document your actual rent, utilities, and food costs and submit that to financial aid. Some schools will adjust your budget.

- The key question is: does your financial aid award total stay the same or does it decrease? If your aid package changes when you switch from on to off campus housing status, that gap matters a lot. If the total aid stays the same but is just allocated differently, it matters less.

- For students on limited budgets who are deciding between on and off campus: run the full calculation. The cheaper rent off campus can be offset by lower financial aid. At some schools on-campus can end up being net cheaper depending on your specific aid package.

- Off campus students at most universities can still purchase a meal plan from the dining hall. It's usually optional rather than required. So "off campus" doesn't mean you lose all access to dining — it just means you're no longer required to buy in.

- The difference at my school was about $7,000 in the COA. But my actual off-campus rent was $400/month in a college town, so my real annual housing cost was $3,600/year vs $8,000 on campus. Even after accounting for the aid difference, I came out ahead significantly. Do the full math for your specific situation.

- Worth talking directly to your financial aid office about this. Explain your situation — living independently, qualifying for extra grants based on that status. They may have specific guidance for independent students about which housing classification gives you better aid.
