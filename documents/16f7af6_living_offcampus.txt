Title: Living offcampus

Post body:
Just moved off campus for the first time this semester. What do I wish I knew? What surprised you about the experience? Sharing to help other people who are about to do the same.

Comments:

- The biggest surprise for me was how much administrative friction there is that you just don't have on campus. Setting up internet, getting the gas turned on, figuring out trash pickup day, understanding what your lease covers for maintenance — it's not hard but it's a lot of small things that nobody prepares you for.

- I wish someone had told me that the "cheap" apartments are cheap for a reason. Our $450/person place had a broken oven for the first two months, a landlord who screened calls, and a mouse problem in October. Saving $100/month wasn't worth it.

- First grocery shop after moving off campus was a wake-up call. I spent $180 and realized I had no idea what things cost because I'd been on a meal plan for two years. The learning curve on feeding yourself on a budget is real. Rice, beans, eggs, and frozen vegetables became my best friends.

- Renter's insurance is like $10-15/month and nobody gets it until they need it. Get it. Your laptop, bike, and anything valuable in that apartment aren't covered by the landlord's policy. I found this out when someone broke into our place junior year.

- The freedom is real and it's great. Nobody cares if I come home at 2am, nobody checks if my room is clean, I can have friends over whenever. After dorm life it felt like a completely different existence. Just make sure you're mature enough to not abuse it.

- Missing your last bus home is a real thing that happened to me in February when it was 14 degrees out. Know the Broome County Transit schedule and BU's shuttle routes cold, or have an Uber budget, or have a friend with a car. The Vestal area is not walkable at night in winter.

- I didn't realize how much the heating situation would matter. Our landlord controls the heat in our building and they keep it at 68 which is way too cold for Binghamton winters. We bought space heaters which cost us money in electricity. Always ask who controls the heat before you sign.

- The social adjustment is real. On campus you run into people constantly. Off campus you have to be intentional about making plans. I got a bit isolated first semester until I started scheduling stuff more deliberately.

- Make a shared cleaning schedule with your roommates the first week and stick to it. We let it slide for a month and then it felt too awkward to bring up and our apartment was kind of disgusting by November. Start the habit early.

- Utilities were more than I expected. We pay electric and gas separately, plus internet. In summer electric was $40/person but in January it hit $95/person just for heat. Budget conservatively: I'd say assume $100-150/person/month in utilities at a Binghamton-area apartment.

- One thing I love: having a real address. Getting packages without dealing with the mail room, having my own space, being able to have a cat (if your lease allows). It just feels more grown-up in a good way.

- If you're moving in August, the apartment usually isn't spotless from the previous tenants. Do a walkthrough with your landlord and document every single thing that's already damaged or dirty with photos and timestamps before you move anything in. This protects your security deposit.

- The commute seemed fine when I moved in but by February when I was walking in slush and 20-degree weather I started reconsidering. If you have any flexibility, being close enough to campus to walk is worth paying slightly more.

- I thought living with my best friends would be amazing. It's good, but it's also revealed that we have genuinely different standards for cleanliness and noise. We worked it out but you learn a lot about people when you live with them.
